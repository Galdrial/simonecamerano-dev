My Story

How I (almost) became a developer (Spoiler: It wasn’t planned)

I’ll be honest: I didn’t wake up at ten years old knowing I’d become a web developer. Sure, as a student at a classical high school, people considered me a nerd because instead of learning Latin and Greek, I spent my days staring at the screen, trying to figure out how GNU/Linux worked.

Unfortunately, I've never had the chance to choose my job or even my university. Life led me to start working very early, and for more than twenty years I was part of the vast world of Large-Scale Retail, covering both operational, as a Responsable, and commercial roles. I had my satisfactions, of course, but I always felt something wasn’t going as I wished.

Now, in my forties, I feel it’s finally time for me to build something that truly represents and fulfills me, both as a worker and as a person.

The idea of diving into Web Development wasn’t sudden but grew over time, until a few months ago I started studying the main programming languages, first self-taught (thank you, YouTube!) and then through more structured courses.
Python was my first love—it seemed simple and intuitive, within my reach. In a few months, by adding Django to my skills, I managed to deploy a small site, maybe trivial, but extremely rewarding.

I was trying to launch a small project (which, by the way, never took off), but between YouTube tutorials and nights of “just one more tweak before bed,” before I knew it, I wasn’t just fixing that site—I was rebuilding it from scratch. And I loved every minute, between frustration and excitement.

What won me over wasn’t just solving problems. It was the creative power. With code, I could turn abstract ideas into real, functional experiences. I could create a button that surprises users. I could optimize loading times and literally save people time. I could build something that didn’t exist yesterday.

Now I want to become a full-stack developer to combine design and functionality. I want to write clean code that makes sense to people, not just machines. I believe the best solutions are invisible and that users shouldn’t notice the technology, just enjoy the experience.

Why do I do it? Because every project is a new challenge, every bug is a puzzle, and every deploy is that moment of “wow, I really built this.”

Technology changes every day, and that’s exactly why I’m here—ready and determined to get back in the game, because I love learning, adapting, and building things that matter.

Values & Work Philosophy

How I work (and why it really matters)

My philosophy is simple: build things that matter.

Sounds basic, right? But here’s what it really means in practice:

QUALITY IS NON-NEGOTIABLE
I don’t deliver half-baked solutions. If my name is on it, it has to really work. Not “works on my machine,” but “tested, optimized, and bulletproof.” I’d rather spend an extra day doing it right than weeks fixing it later.

USERS ARE PEOPLE, NOT NUMBERS
Every button I code, every form I design, there’s a real person on the other side. Maybe they’re tired, in a hurry, or using their phone with one hand while holding coffee with the other one. I code for that reality, not for perfect conditions that don’t exist.

THE TEAM IS STRONGER THAN THE INDIVIDUAL
I used to think the best developers were soloists. I was wrong. The best work happens when developers, designers, and stakeholders really talk to each other. I ask questions, share doubts early, and celebrate wins with the team because great products aren’t built alone.

LEARNING NEVER STOPS
Technology evolves. What was cutting-edge last year is already old. I stay curious, read documentation, try new tools, and admit when I don’t know something—then I learn it. Ego is useless in development.

What makes me unique? I care about the code, the user, the team, and the result. I don’t just check boxes; I build solutions that really solve problems.
